Title Equitable access to satellite broadband services: Challenges and opportunities for developing countries
Citation
Credibility Dr Akcali Gur is the module convenor for Outer Space Law at the Centre for Commercial Law Studies.

Her research focuses on public international law, world trade law, and EU Law implications of advancements in information and communication technologies.
Summary This source emphasizes the connection between the rise of centralized satellite broadband in the control of the few and the access developing countries have to the internet. It highlights the risk of allowing a few stakeholders to hold the reins for entire megaconstellations, affecting affordability and access which can change across geopolitical regions.
Relevance This source just strengthens the argument that big tech monopolization of internet infrastructure may provide overall access but doesn't ensure that the access is equitable and secure, in some cases actively endangering this access later on.

(Equitable access to satellite broadband services, p.8)
At this nascent stage of satellite broadband technology, where prominent examples in the sector are mostly based on one leading company, SpaceX and its Starlink satellite constellation, the drawbacks of privatized Internet governance are perceived to outweigh the benefits (Kradomski, 2022). The prospect of critical communication infrastructures being subject to the decisions of a single individual is understandably viewed as a risk. These companies are operating globally and extending their services across jurisdictions through partnerships with other private companies across the connectivity ecosystem and governments that provide connectivity as a public service. Considering that this is a critical infrastructure, foreign dependence is already perceived as a challenge, even if its control is not left to one person.
