She is also a storyteller and a trained journalist with more than 15 years of experience in the media sector. Summary This source explains how internet infrastructure works and how the big tech domination of the internet and everyday services impacts users from the Global North when compared to the Global South. When users request content, that request is either sent via direct connection between your internet service provider (ISP) and a company's content delivery network (CDN). However, the speed, cost, and latency of this connection depends entirely on where the CDN is located, and how many public "interconnection" points the company has opened in the physical world. Big Tech's dominance over the internet and subsequently all services embedded or hosted within means that if these connections are weak, users and endpoint businesses will lose out on the ability to compete properly in the industry.

The source points out that Big Tech has situated significantly more interconnection points in Western countries when compared to the Global South, despite their populations overshadowing the North. This inequality is due to Big Tech's monopolization of everything digital, forcing the Global South to pay the required fees to get access to Big Tech's services - without which they will fall behind.

Citation

Summary This source highlights a threat that broadband satellites monopolies introduce when they become "unavoidable intermediaries" of internet access. With so much power, the monopolizing entity holds geopolitical power to cut access to the Internet at any location they want, allowing them to censor and hold complete control over the flow of information.
Relevance This source readily disagrees with the argument that Big Tech control over digital infrastructure is a net benefit because it strengthens resilience.
